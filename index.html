Filing a civil suit involves drafting a plaint with relevant facts and relief sought, paying the requisite court fees, filing before the appropriate court having jurisdiction, and serving notice to the defendant. The process includes stages like written statement, framing of issues, evidence, arguments, and judgment. An advocate can guide you through each step.

The duration of a criminal trial varies depending on the complexity of the case, the court's workload, number of witnesses, and nature of charges. Simple cases may conclude within months, while complex matters can take several years. Fast-track courts and special courts expedite certain categories of cases. Strategic legal representation can help navigate the process efficiently.

RERA (Real Estate Regulatory Authority) is a statutory body established under the Real Estate (Regulation and Development) Act, 2016 to protect homebuyers and regulate the real estate sector. An advocate can help file complaints for delayed possession, seek refunds with interest, ensure project registration compliance, and represent clients before RERA authorities and appellate tribunals.

The National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT) is a quasi-judicial body that adjudicates issues relating to companies. It handles matters including insolvency and bankruptcy proceedings under IBC, oppression and mismanagement claims, mergers and amalgamations, winding up of companies, and disputes between members and the company. Legal representation before NCLT requires specialized knowledge of company law.

A lawyer can conduct comprehensive trademark searches to ensure your mark is unique, file the application with the Trademark Registry, respond to examination reports and objections, represent you at hearings, and handle opposition proceedings. Post-registration, a lawyer can assist with renewals, assignments, and enforcement against infringement.

Litigation is dispute resolution through courts and follows the Civil Procedure Code, while arbitration is an alternative dispute resolution (ADR) mechanism where parties agree to have their dispute decided by an arbitrator. Arbitration is generally faster, more flexible, confidential, and the award is binding and enforceable. It is governed by the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 in India.

Bail can be sought at various stages: anticipatory bail (before arrest) under Section 438 CrPC, regular bail (after arrest) under Section 437/439 CrPC, or default bail (if chargesheet is not filed within the prescribed period). The application is filed before the appropriate court with grounds for bail, surety details, and relevant case facts. Prompt legal assistance is crucial in bail matters.

For incorporating a company in India, you need: PAN and Aadhaar of all directors, address proof and passport-size photographs of directors, Digital Signature Certificates (DSC), Director Identification Numbers (DIN), proposed company name approvals, registered office address proof, Memorandum and Articles of Association, and declarations from first directors and subscribers.

FEMA (Foreign Exchange Management Act) violations include unauthorized foreign exchange dealings, non-repatriation of foreign exchange, holding foreign exchange beyond permissible limits, and non-compliance with reporting requirements. Consequences include penalties up to three times the amount involved, adjudication proceedings, and in severe cases, criminal prosecution. Compounding of offences is available for certain contraventions.

Money recovery options include: sending a legal notice demanding payment, filing a recovery suit or summary suit in civil court, approaching the Debt Recovery Tribunal (for amounts above ₹20 lakhs), initiating insolvency proceedings under IBC (for amounts above ₹1 crore), filing under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act for cheque bounce cases, or pursuing arbitration if an arbitration clause exists.
